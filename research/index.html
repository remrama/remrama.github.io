Broadly, I'm interested in how cognition fluctuates across the circadian cycle. Wake and sleep are more complicated than on and off. To better understand how we retain mental information generally, I study working memory in waking humans. The best demonstration, I think, of how sleep is not a strictly "off" state comes from lucid dreaming. When someone becomes lucid in their dream, they regain waking levels of mental awareness and determine correctly that they are dreaming as the dream is occurring (i.e., without waking up). The goal of my work is to study mental faculties across the entire circadian cycle to uncover the building blocks of human cognition.

Below I've grouped my research projects into these two broad categories.


Working memory research

Working memory, attention, and perception

Perception and working memory recruit similar neural systems. A red stop sign will grab your perceptual attention if you are looking for a friend in a red coat. But we hold more than one item in mind at any given time, and it is unclear as to how switching internal attention between memories impacts the way they influence perception. By applying machine-learning techniques to behavioral data, we showed that this is not the case for all memory items. By temporarily switching internal attention away from a memory item, it no longer interacts with the environment.

Distraction recovery

We use working memory all day, at which time we are also bombarded with incoming perceptual information. How does the brain do both simultaneously? What prevents perception from interrupting or even overwriting mnemonic information? We found that directing internal attention towards or away from mental contents influences the amount of disruption caused by a distracting task.


Lucid dreaming research

Phenomenology

What is it like to be lucid? Just like wake/sleep is not simply on/off, one is not simply lucid or not. Rather, it falls on some type of spectrum. We asked participants after awakening about how lucid their dream was, and also followups about why they reported their dream as such. This work was recently presented as a poster at the 2019 IASD conference, and the manuscript is currently in prep.

Induction

Perhaps the largest roadblock to the use of lucid dreaming in research is its infrequency. Thus, the most immediate goal in lucid dreaming research is to develop a reliable method of inducing lucid dreams. To this aim, we have developed a novel induction method that aggregates previous methods that have shown to be moderately effective individually. We found that when associating an audio/visual cue with mindful awareness immediately before a nap, replaying the cue during sleep induces a lucid dream about 50% of the time.

Communication

Since lucid dreamers are aware they are dreaming, they are also aware of the waking or "outside" world while they are asleep. Though surrounded by internal mental contents, lucid dreamers can try to communicate with a waking person. Though muscle atonia leaves most of the sleeping body paralyzed, actions of the dreamer often result in subtle muscle twitches which can be read out with polysomnography or other measures. My Bachelor's thesis was a project where a few participants tried to control a consumer brain-computer interface during a lucid dream. And it kinda worked! Our newer work revolves around a closed-loop device that can detect intentional eye signaling from a lucid dream and respond to the dreamer with audio.
